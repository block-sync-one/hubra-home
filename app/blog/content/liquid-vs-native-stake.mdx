Basics of Staking

Staking on the Solana network is an important part of the process, where SOL token holders take part in the consensus process and get rewards for doing so. This process involves giving some or all of your tokens to certain validators, which gives them more power to vote without giving up your ownership or control of the tokens.

You trust the validator you chose because you gave them the power to act on your behalf. When validators get stakes from more than one tokenholder, this shows the network that their consensus votes are trustworthy. The network includes this delegated stake in its consensus mechanism, which means that validators with a larger delegated stake have more say in how decisions are made.

We will talk about the details of SOL staking in the next sections, including its two main types: native staking and liquid staking. People who want to use Solana's network effectively and get the most out of their SOL assets need to understand these ideas.

What Does Native Staking Mean?

Native staking is an important part of the SOL staking ecosystem that keeps the Solana blockchain running smoothly and safely. In native staking, people who own SOL tokens choose to give a certain amount of their tokens to a specific validator. This gives the validator more voting power in the network. This process requires locking up your SOL tokens for a set amount of time so that the network can work properly.

### Pros and Cons of Native Staking

Benefits ✓

- **Improved Network Security:** Native staking makes the network safer by giving validators a reason to act in the best interest of the system.
- **Helping Choose Validators:** Token holders have the right to choose validators they trust and believe in, which helps decentralization.
- **Staking Rewards:** Native staking offers attractive rewards that allow participants to earn passive income from their staked tokens.
- **Community Participation:** People who stake their SOL tokens help run the network and make decisions.
- **Improved Token Value:** Staking lowers the number of tokens in circulation, which could raise the token's market value over time.

The Bad Things ✗

- **Lock-Up Period:** During the staking period, tokens are locked up and can't be traded or used for other purposes (about 2.5 days on average).
- **Possibility of Poor Validator Performance:** If a validator doesn't do their job well or acts maliciously, it could also hurt the interests of stakeholders.
- **Progression of Competency:** Users may need to know about a variety of technical and network-related things in order to stake.

What is Liquid Staking?

Liquid staking is a new step in the staking paradigm that gives token owners more flexibility and liquidity. In short, liquid staking means spreading your SOL tokens among different validators, keeping an eye on your stake, and moving it around as needed, such as when a validator stops working. A new idea called pledging derivatives makes this progress possible. Staking derivatives are digital tokens that hold the value of pledged assets. This lets users get the benefits of staking without having to lock in their assets for a set amount of time.

The Pros and Cons of Liquid Staking

Pros ✓

- **Stake Rewards:** With liquid staking, you can keep getting staking rewards without having to lock up your assets. - No Lock-Up Periods: You can get to and use your assets whenever you want, without having to wait for lock-up periods to end.
- More Flexibility: Participants can decide how to use their staked assets, which gives them access to a wide range of DeFi protocols and services.
- **Chance for Higher Returns:** Liquid staking platforms often offer good interest rates, which lets you earn rewards.
- **Lowered Thresholds for Participation:** Newcomers can join stake and decentralized finance projects more easily without having to invest a lot of money.

Problems ✗

- **Market Volatility:** The native SOL price and the stake rewards that go with it usually show that an investment is liquid. It could be a chance for the Liquid Stake Tokens (LST) to go up in value, depending on how much they are worth on their own. - **ephemeral loss:** Putting money into liquidity pools can cause ephemeral loss, which can change the total value of your holdings.
- **Risk of Security Breaches and Exploits:** Liquid staking platforms may be open to smart contract bugs and cyberattacks.
- **Trustworthiness of the Platform:** Choosing a reliable liquid staking platform is important to lower risks and make sure your assets are safe.

## Native vs. Liquid: Important Things to Think About

Native staking and liquid staking are two different ways to get involved in blockchain networks, and each has its own pros and cons. Native staking means locking up your cryptocurrency holdings to help the network run smoothly. This is a safe method. On the other hand, liquid staking lets you keep your assets liquid and accessible while also earning rewards. Both options have their own pros and cons, and you should choose the one that best fits your financial goals, risk tolerance, and personal preferences.

### Main Things to Think About

- **Risk Tolerance:** Think about how comfortable you are with the possibility of asset values going up and down and the need for liquidity.
- **Investment Goals:** Clearly state your financial goals and the time frame you expect to invest.
- **Rewards:** Look at the possible rewards and interest rates for each method.
- **Choosing Platforms:** Do a full analysis of the different platforms that support both native and liquid stake features.
- **Tax Consequences:** Be aware of the tax implications of pledging.
- **Regulatory Considerations:** Be aware of the rules and regulations that apply to your area.
